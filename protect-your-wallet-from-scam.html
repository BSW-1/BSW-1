How to Keep Your eSewa and Khalti Safe from “Remote Access” Scams: The 2026 Ultimate Security Guide

By Bhaskar Sah | January 28, 2026 | Cyber Security

The digital revolution in Nepal has brought immense convenience. From paying for electricity bills through the Nepal Electricity Authority (NEA) and internet bills to Worldlink or Vianet, to fund transfers within seconds from any corners of a country, all of this has become possible because of digital wallets like eSewa, Khalti and IME Pay. But by 2026, this convenience will have a hefty catch. Nepal has seen a staggering 40% surge in cyber-enabled financial crimes in the last fiscal year alone.

The most dangerous threat today isn't a complex hack into the wallet’s high-security servers it is the "Remote Access Scam." In this comprehensive 2026 guide, we will break down the mechanics of these scams, explain why traditional 2FA is being bypassed, and provide the exact technical steps you must take to shield your hard-earned money from digital predators.

1. Understanding the Crisis: Why Digital Fraud is Peaking in Nepal

By early 2025, and transitioning into 2026, more than thousand accounts specifically concerned "Account Takeover" were reported to Nepal Police Cyber Bureau. Now, they don’t just ask you to hand over your password; they’re playing mind games in a bid for access to full control of your entire smartphone.

1.1. Why Nepal has become a Prime Target:

  • Rapid Adoption vs. Digital Literacy: Millions of Nepalese have transitioned to eSewa and Khalti for daily transactions, but many are unaware of the technical risks associated with "Screen Sharing" and "Permission Requests."
  • The Power of Social Engineering: Scammers have mastered the local language and use "Urgency" as a weapon. They create panic by claiming your account will be blocked in 2 hours or that your bank link has a technical error.
  • The SIM-Swap Evolution: By gaining access to your SIM card or viewing your screen remotely, attackers can bypass the security layers designed by Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB). When the thief can see what you see, your security becomes an open book.

2. Anatomy of a "Remote Access" Scam

This is the most critical topic for 2026 because it represents the highest volume of financial loss reported to the authorities. Understanding the workflow of a scammer is the first step toward prevention.

Phase 1: The Phishing Call

You get a call from someone purporting to be an "eSewa Support Agent," "Khalti KYC Officer" and, in some cases, even from the Nepal Rastra Bank. These types of scammers commonly use something called Caller ID Spoofing -- this is where the number they are calling from typically shows up on your phone as a company official hotline and/or a land line # in Kathmandu.

Phase 2: The Manufactured Problem

They claim your KYC (Know Your Customer) is expired, or a "suspicious transaction of Rs. 25,000" was detected from your account. They play on your fear of losing money to make you follow their instructions without thinking.

Phase 3: The Instrument of Destruction

They’ll ask you to download a “Support App” from the Play Store to assist you in resolving the problem. They usually mention trustworthy remote desktop software such as AnyDesk, TeamViewer or RustDesk. Since these apps are on the official Play Store, users tend to trust them without knowing they are “giving away the keys to their house.”

Phase 4: The Total Takeover

Once you provide the "Remote ID" or "Access Code," the scammer can see your screen in real-time. They can see your messages, your gallery, and your banking apps.

The 2FA Bypass:
Now, even if you have Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) turned on, you’re not safe this way either. As the scamming is done with the scammer staring at your screen through AnyDesk, when you receive the OTP over SMS, they see it on their side immediately as well.

3. Beyond OTP: Advanced Tactics Used by Modern Fraudsters

Modern fraudsters in Nepal are using advanced tactics that most basic blogs don't cover yet. As we navigate 2026, you must be aware of these three sophisticated methods:

A. Overlay Attacks

Scammers may trick you into installing a "Rewards" app. This app hides in the background and places a fake "Login Screen" over your real Khalti or eSewa app. When you think you are typing your MPIN into your wallet, you are actually typing it into the scammer’s invisible form.

B. Notification Mirroring

Some malicious apps (disguised as "Cashback" or "Free Data" apps) ask for "Notification Access." This is a dangerous permission. It allows the scammer to read your SMS OTPs and bank alerts without you even opening the messaging app. They can delete the notification after reading it, so you don't even know an OTP was generated.

C. Social Media "Help" Scams

Scammers join popular Facebook groups like “eSewa Help & Support” (unofficial) and watch for desperate users to post about failed or botched transactions. They follow up with a DM (Direct Message) under the disguise of an Official account and then let the user install RATS as described above.

4. How to Protect Your Online Accounts?

As per the recent guidelines by Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) Payment System Department, user is responsible for sharing their credentials. To protect your money, use these non-negotiable security procedures:

A. Enable Biometric Locks (Fingerprint/Face ID)

MPINs (4-digit pins) are vulnerable. They can be seen through screen sharing, captured by keyloggers, or seen by someone looking over your shoulder ("shoulder surfing"). Biometrics, however, require your physical presence.

Action: Go to Settings in your eSewa/Khalti app and enable Biometric Authentication for Login & Transactions respectively. Never rely on a PIN alone.

B. The "No-Remote" Rule

This is the golden rule of digital safety. Never, not ever, download a screen-sharing app because someone on the phone asked you to.

Fact: No genuine company who operates in Nepal which includes eSewa, Khalti, IME Pay or any commercial bank will ever tell you to install AnyDesk/TeamViewer allowing them to support you.

C. Use "App Lock" Specifically for SMS

If your phone is stolen or access while not locked, the thief's first action will be to ask for an OTP. By using a second password or fingerprint to lock the codes inside your “Messages” and “Email” apps, you add a second layer of defense that prevents them from ever seeing the code.

D. Audit Your App Permissions

Audit Your App Permissions Visit your phone’s Settings > Privacy > Permission Manager. Search for apps with “SMS Access” or “Notification Access.” If there is, and the app doesn’t have to have access for its basic functioning (say, a calculator or wallpaper app), yank the permission immediately.

E. Do not Trust any Confirmed News Only Believe on Official Channels

If you get a call that seems suspicious, just hang up. Do not use the number from which they called you. Instead, directly dial the official toll-free numbers:

  • eSewa Official Support: 1660-01-02121
  • Khalti Official Support: 1660-01-58888
  • Cyber Bureau Nepal: 01-4219044

5. Response to Emergencies: What You Can Do if you are a Victim of Fraud?

If you know you’ve been had, or see transactions that were unauthorized, each second becomes a race to salvage your remaining funds. Follow this protocol:

  1. Freeze the Account: Immediately call your wallet provider from a different phone to "Freeze" your ID. This prevents further outgoing transfers.
  2. Contact the Bank: If your wallet is connected to any of Bank ( Siddhartha, Global IME, Nabil, NIC Asia, Everest etc) then you can immediately call bank’s 24/7 Hotline and ask them to disable “Link Account” or “Mobile Banking” feature.
  3. Report to the Cyber Bureau: Go to the Nepal Police Cyber Bureau located at Bhotahity, Kathmandu. Give them the transaction ID, scammer’s phone number and screenshots of the conversation. You may also submit a complaint online through their website.
  4. Format Your Phone: If you have downloaded AnyDesk or the APK file, since installing it, the scammer might still have access. Perform factory reset of your phone in order to be sure, no trace of malware left.

6. Conclusion: A Constant Learning Process

The "Digital Wallet Security Crisis" in Nepal is a battle of awareness. Fintechs such as eSewa and Khalti provide the base for infrastructure – but when it comes to security, that’s in the hands of users. The methods of crooks are only going to become more realistic as 2026 unfolds, perhaps even adopting AI-created voices that impersonate bank managers.

The first step is not to leave remote access enabled, then embracing biometrics, and remaining healthily suspicious of ‘Support Calls,’ you can still get the best out of digital Nepal without the risk of financial doom. Because your security is a process, not a product.
