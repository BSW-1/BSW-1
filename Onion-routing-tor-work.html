Onion Routing & Anonymity Networks A Deep Dive into Tor, I2P, and Online Privacy

By Bhaskar Sah | December 21, 2025 | Dark Web
Quick Summary: Onion routing When you use onion routing, you bundle your data in layers of encryption and send it bouncing to random computers (relays) scattered around the globe. It’s a digital shell game that helps keep your identity and location hidden from snoopers.

1. What “onion routing” means

Onion routing is a networking technique designed to protect who you are and where you are connecting from.

The idea:

  • Your message is wrapped in multiple layers of encryption (like layers of an onion).
  • Each network relay removes only one layer.
  • No single relay knows both:
    • Who you are, and
    • Where your data is ultimately going

Core goal

Prevent any observer (ISPs, governments, websites, attackers) from easily linking:

User Activity Destination

2. How Tor works (The Onion Router) ?

The best known onion routing network is Tor. It was actually created by the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory.

2.1 Tor’s relay system

When you’re on Tor, your traffic goes across at least three relays:

  • Entry (Guard) Node
    • Knows who you are (your IP).
    • Does not know what site you’re visiting.
  • Middle Relay
    • Passes encrypted traffic along.
    • Knows neither the sender nor the destination.
  • Exit Node
    • Knows the destination (the website you want).
    • Does not know who you are.

Each relay can only see its previous and next hop, never the full path.

2.2 Encryption layers (the “onion”)

Before your data leaves your device:

  1. It is encrypted three times.
  2. Each relay peels off one layer.
  3. Only the final destination sees the fully decrypted request.

This prevents traffic correlation by a single observer.

2.3 Tor hidden services (.onion)

Some websites exist inside the Tor network and never touch the regular internet. These are often called Onion services or .onion sites.

How they differ:

  • The user’s location is hidden.
  • The server’s location is also hidden.
  • Traffic never exits Tor.

How is this not a VPN??

Tor is often mistaken for VPNs. Here is the difference:

3. I2P (Invisible Internet Project)

You mentioned “12P” — this is likely I2P.

Key differences from Tor

Feature Tor I2P
Focus Anonymously accessing the web Anonymous internal services
Traffic Mostly outbound Mostly peer-to-peer
Routing Circuit-based Garlic routing
Use case Browsing, anonymity Distributed apps, messaging

Garlic routing (I2P)

Instead of one message = one onion:

  • Multiple messages are bundled together ("cloves" in a garlic).
  • This increases resistance to traffic analysis.
  • Makes it harder to correlate timing patterns.

4. How anonymity networks protect users (technically)

4.1 Threats they defend against

  • IP address tracking: Websites do not see your real IP.
  • ISP surveillance: Your Internet Provider can see that you are using Tor, but not what for.
  • Network-level censorship: Getting around companies in constrained countries.

4.2 What they do not protect against

Anonymity networks cannot protect you if:

  • You log into your accounts (like Facebook, Google).
  • You download infected software or files.
  • You reveal personal info voluntarily in chats.
  • Your device itself is compromised with malware.

Remember: Anonymity ≠ Invisibility

5. Common misconceptions

  • ☛ “Tor is only for criminals”
    False. Many legitimate uses exist: Journalists, Activists, Researchers, and people living under censorship.

  • ☛ “Tor makes you untraceable”
    False. It reduces risk significantly but is not a magic shield against dedicated nation-state attackers.

  • ☛ “The Dark Web is a different internet”
    Not really. It’s still the internet, just accessed through different routing protocols.

6. Safety considerations (important)

Since you asked about safety, here are conceptual rules, not instructions:

Technical safety

  • Anonymity depends on behavior, not just tools.
  • Browser exploits (JavaScript) can bypass network protections.
  • Metadata (timing, size, patterns) still matters.

Personal safety

  • Not all hidden services are trustworthy.
  • Some content can be harmful or illegal.
  • Curiosity without context can expose you to risks.

7. Ethical use of anonymity networks

Anonymity networks are dual-use technologies.

Ethical uses include:
  • Protecting free speech
  • Avoiding political persecution
  • Researching sensitive topics
  • Secure communication
Unethical uses include:
  • Harassment
  • Scams
  • Exploitation
  • Coordinating harm

Ethical principle:

Use anonymity to protect rights — not to remove responsibility.

8. Why this matters today

Onion routing represents a bigger idea:

  • Privacy as a technical property
  • Power balance between users and observers
  • Resistance to mass surveillance

Glossary of Terms

Node / Relay
A computer that participates in the Tor network by passing traffic along.

Bridge
A secret node not publicly listed, for when some would-be Tor users have their network connections blocked in countries such as China or Iran.

Fingerprinting
Tricks they use to track you include your browser settings, screen size and fonts – and even if your IP address is hidden.

By learning how it works, you can critically evaluate privacy claims, identify trade-offs and make ethical decisions about technology.

Conclusion

Privacy-enhanced network architectures (such as onion routing and the various anonymity networks – Tor, I2P) offer evidence that privacy can be an intrinsic part of a network design. Not by decoupling identity from destination (as with Tor) or by trusting the system operator not to spy on every user, but rather by separating what any one observer can see. At the same time they are not magic shields: user behavior, device security and ethical decisions still count.Comprehending the operation - and consequently its limitations - of these particular technologies are among the most effective ways for users to understand how to handle them responsibly, the extent of their ability to protect user privacy, and their importance in the protection of the rights to free expression and access to information in an increasingly monitored online ecosystem.
